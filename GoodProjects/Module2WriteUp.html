The Model:

The selection of our model is best explained by an examination into the dataset that we have been provided. The easily noticeable trend in the data collected so far  is that the majority of clients that we called responded with a "no" instead of a "yes". This creates an inherent bias in any model because we don't have as much if formation about those who responded “yes”. To remedy this we used a technique called "Oversampling", which duplicated the past values in the “yes” section to change the ratio of responses in the dataset and create a 50/50 split. This helped our decision tree model to learn what influences someone’s decision to respond positively to our calls, rather than just learning how likely someone is to respond negatively to our advances.

Validation Score: 94.68%

It is important to note that the metric for validation also includes our models' correct predictions for the category of customers who will respond negatively to our calls. When we tested the validation on our oversampled dataset we found that the model struggled to predict the “yes” value much more than the “no” values. The “yes” value was predicted correctly less than half of the time, while the “no” value was still predicted correctly almost all the time. Ways that we hope to improve our model in the future are included in the “actionable items” section.

Our Decisions:

        Since we used the oversampling method, we were able to accurately have our machine learning model predict the outcome, whether it be “yes” or “no” to whether or not they have had a term deposit. Our accuracy was good, coming in at 80% respectively. What we really used to determine our results was the recall, which describes out of all of the people that have had term deposits, the percentage that our model predicts would have one. When using the oversampling method as previously stated, we were able to get our recall percentage to be 69%, which is a good statistic.

When running an evaluation set of data through our model, we were able to successfully identify 295 of the 427 actual “yes” responses for a recall of 69%. This is our most relevant statistic, as the consequence of falsely predicting a “yes” and calling someone who rejects the campaign is not as bad as missing a person who would have actually said yes. Out of our validation set, the model only falsely predicted 603 positive responses out of the total 3280 predicted positives, yielding a precision of 86.3%.

“Does it make sense to call students at all?”

As you can see, the students are almost 50/50 on who said yes and who said no, which is a lot higher than most other jobs. Out of any demographic that we have, we should keep reaching out to students.

“Does contacting people too frequently for these marketing campaigns have an adverse effect on the outcome? “

No: we ran a statistical t-test to compare whether or not people who responded favorably and unfavorably based on how many days since they’d been contacted. With a p-value score of 0.2434, we can safely assume that it is probably not significant. As shown by the graphs below, both graphs have the same shape, so the number of days did not correlate in a change of success rate.

II.       Beatriz: Data Privacy

Under the European Union’s GDPR, this type of analysis should be fine. This algorithm doesn’t reject people applying, only determines who might want to, so there are no legal effects. From Recital 71 of the guidelines:

“The data subject should have the right not to be subject to a decision, which may include a measure, evaluating personal aspects relating to him or her which is based solely on automated processing and which produces legal effects concerning him or her or similarly significantly affects him or her, such as automatic refusal of an online credit application or e-recruiting practices without any human intervention”

This machine learning algorithm is acceptable.
