Residuals

However, not every day from last week matched the temperature wed expect based on our model. In fact, none of them do! The distance between each actual value and what we would expect is called a residual. \[Residual = Y_i - \hat{Y}_i\]

ggplot(rex,aes(x=daysaway,y=high)) + geom_point() +
  geom_smooth(method='lm',se=FALSE,formula=y~x) + theme_bw() +
  labs(y='High Daily Temperature',x='Day of the Month',title='High Residual = Sad Predictions') + xlim(1,9) + ylim(15,40) + scale_x_continuous(breaks=seq(1,9,by=1)) +
  geom_segment(aes(x=6,xend=6,y=34,yend=31.2),size=3)

Youll notice that none of the residuals are very big. In fact, the way we find our regression in the first place is by trying to make those as small as possible, so every point is relatively close to our expected value. A little more specifically, it gives extra weight to points that are already far away. The way it does that is by choosing not to minimize the total distance (which might put 4 really close and one really far away), but by minimizing the square of the distance (which might put all 5 kinda close).

SSE

ggplot(rex,aes(x=daysaway,y=high)) + geom_point() + geom_point(x=9,y=34.92857,size=3,color='green') +
  geom_smooth(method='lm',se=FALSE,formula=y~x) + theme_bw() +
  labs(y='High Daily Temperature',x='Day of the Month',title='Squaring the Residual') + xlim(1,9) + ylim(15,40) +
  geom_rect(aes(xmin=daysaway,xmax=daysaway-rex.lm$residuals*.15,ymin=high,ymax=rex.lm$fitted.values),alpha=0.1) + scale_x_continuous(breaks=seq(1,9,by=1))

If we add up all of these squares, the number we get is called the Sum of Squared Error (or SSE). In math we can write this as \[SSE = \sum_{i=1}^n (Y_i - \hat{Y}_i)^2\] Youll recognize the middle part from our residual, which just means were squaring each of those and then adding all of them together. Its a great way of judging whether or not your regression is close to your data. If SSE is small, that means your residuals are small, and your regression is a good, tight fit. The best it can be is 0, which means our regression is perfect.

Lets Ignore the Dots Now

Now that we have our model, we can also make a general comparison to the whole of our dataset. If we had no x-axis, no explanatory variable, the best prediction we could make is by guessing the mean. This is \(\bar{Y}\), because on the graph it looks like a bar stretching across. If this was the best we had, it wouldnt matter if we tried to make a linear regression because it would be no better than a plan ol average. So how much better is a regression? Lets compare! (Mean is Maroon)

ggplot(rex,aes(x=daysaway,y=high)) + geom_point() +
  geom_smooth(method='lm',se=FALSE,formula=y~x) + theme_bw() +
  labs(y='High Daily Temperature',x='Day of the Month',title='Residual v Mean: Dawn of Justice') + xlim(1,9) + ylim(15,40) +
  geom_segment(aes(x=1,xend=9,y=mean(high),yend=mean(high)),color='maroon') +  
  geom_segment(aes(x=daysaway,xend=daysaway,y=mean(high),yend=rex.lm$fitted.values)) +
  scale_x_continuous(breaks=seq(1,7,by=1))

This is similar to our residual, but instead of finding the distance between 1) our observed points and 2) the regression, we can find the distance between each points expected value (on the \(\hat{Y}_i\)) and our mean (\(\bar{Y}\)). If we do the same thing we did to find the SSE (square that distance and then add them together) well find our Sum of Squared Regression (Error), or SSR. \[SSR = \sum_{i=1}^n (\hat{Y}_i - \bar{Y})^2\] This is useful in a different way from what we had before, SSE. That one tells us how bad our model still is (what we still dont understand), while the Sum of Squared Regression tells us how good the model is (how much better it is than a basic guess)

Bringing them Together

Right now we have two ways of measuring: SSE (observed-predicted) and SSR (predicted-average). These are two ways of measuring variation in our regression. Is there another way?

Both SSE and SSR are built from our predicted model, the regression. What if we ignored it? Instead of comparing with our regression, lets see what happens if we just find the deviation of average & observed.

ggplot(rex,aes(x=daysaway,y=high)) + geom_point() +
  theme_bw() + 
  labs(y='High Daily Temperature',x='Day of the Month',title='Easy Math!') + xlim(1,7) + ylim(15,40) +
  geom_segment(aes(x=1,xend=7,y=mean(high),yend=mean(high)),color='maroon') +
  scale_x_continuous(breaks=seq(1,7,by=1))

This graph is essentially a worst-case scenario. We have no new information about the individual data, and all of the deviation is pretty big. It is going to be worse than the SSE, because we havent done any work to try and minimze it. However, if this and the SSE are pretty close to each other, that means the slope of our regression is pretty shallow, because the squares that we are adding together havent changed very much.

ggplot(rex,aes(x=daysaway,y=high)) + geom_point() + theme_bw() +
  labs(y='High Daily Temperature',x='Day of the Month',title='Squaring the Mean') + xlim(1,7) + ylim(15,40) +
  geom_segment(aes(x=1,xend=7,y=mean(high),yend=mean(high)),color='maroon') +
  geom_rect(aes(xmin=daysaway,xmax=daysaway-rex.lm$residuals*.25,ymin=high,ymax=mean(high)),alpha=0.1) + scale_x_continuous(breaks=seq(1,7,by=1))

This highest deviance, this total variation, is called Total Sum of Squares or SSTO. This statistic isnt very valuable on its own, but it becomes the foundation to figuring out exactly how useful our regressions SSE is compared to our basic means SSR. In fact, if we add together our SSE (observed-predicted) + SSR (predicted-mean) we get SSTO (observed-mean)! \[SSR + SSE = SSTO = \sum_{i=1}^n (Y_i - \bar{Y})^2\]

R-Squared

Now, its time to talk about relationships. Earlier we mentioned that if your dots are about as close to the regression as they are to the average, your regression isnt very useful. Now that we have all the pieces together, we can figure out exactly how useful it is. \(R^2\) is a number between 1 and 0 that we can use to describe how much our regression teaches us about the data. More specifically, its the proportion of variability in our y-variable than can be explained by the regression.

\(~\) Both \(\bar{Y}\) and \(\hat{Y}\) are different ways of explaining why there are different values of Y. The purpose of our regression is to add a secondary variable (in this case, the day of the month) that can give some more info on why those y-values might be different. If \(R^2\) is really high, the more separated the regression (\(\hat{Y}\)) and the mean (\(\bar{Y}\)) are visually, which means the regression is doing a lot of explaining.

ggplot(rex,aes(x=daysaway,y=high)) + geom_point() +
  geom_smooth(method='lm',se=FALSE,formula=y~x) + theme_bw() +
  labs(y='High Daily Temperature',x='Day of the Month',title='Big Separation in Lines = Big R-squared') + xlim(1,7) + ylim(15,40) +
  geom_segment(aes(x=1,xend=7,y=mean(high),yend=mean(high)),color='maroon') +   scale_x_continuous(breaks=seq(1,7,by=1))  

  #geom_rect(aes(xmin=daysaway,xmax=daysaway-rex.lm$residuals*.15,ymin=high,ymax=rex.lm$fitted.values),alpha=0.1)

The difference between mean and regression? That sounds familiar, its the SSR! Sum of Squared Regression Error finds the amount of difference between those two lines for each of our data points. So if SSTO represents the total variability in the set, and SSR finds a chunk of that variability, then to find \(R^2\) all we have to do is divide the two!

\[R^2 = \frac{SSR}{SSTO}\]

ggplot(rex,aes(x=daysaway,y=high)) + geom_point() +
  geom_smooth(method='lm',se=FALSE,formula=y~x) + theme_bw() +
  labs(y='High Daily Temperature',x='Day of the Month',title='Predictions in Green!') + xlim(1,7) + ylim(15,40) +
  geom_segment(aes(x=1,xend=7,y=mean(high),yend=mean(high)),color='maroon') +  
  geom_rect(aes(xmin=daysaway,xmax=daysaway-rex.lm$residuals*.15,ymin=high,ymax=rex.lm$fitted.values),alpha=0.1) +
  geom_rect(aes(xmin=daysaway,xmax=daysaway-rex.lm$residuals*.15,ymin=rex.lm$fitted.values,ymax=mean(high)),alpha=0.5) +
  scale_x_continuous(breaks=seq(1,7,by=1))

All the grey together is SSTO, the distance between our data and the mean. The dark grey part shows the SSR, or the portion of that SSTO between our useful regression line and that mean. Finally, \(R^2\) is the proportion of that total grey that is dark. (The leftover grey, the lighter part, is SSE, the part we still cant explain with the regression)

ss <- data.frame(Value = c(sum((rex.lm$fitted.values-mean(rex$high))^2), sum((mean(rex$high)-rex$high)^2), sum((mean(rex$high)-rex$high)^2)-sum((rex.lm$fitted.values-mean(rex$high))^2)) ,
           SquareSums = c('SSE','SSTO','SSR'))
barplot(Value~SquareSums, data=ss,main='SSE/SSTO = R Squared!')

Now just because its a number between 0 and 1 doesnt mean its the same as a p-value. In fact, many times they will be opposite! P-value is looking at the relationship between the regression line and the mean. Are they different? P-value tells you how likely it is that the regression model is useful: low means it has a use, high means you might as well just predict future values off of the mean. \(R^2\), on the other hand, tells us HOW useful the regression is, assuming that it is useful. Most common is that you will have a high p-value with a low \(R^2\) (not much better than the mean and not very useful), or a low p-value and a high \(R^2\) (better than the mean, and also useful), although there can be exceptions.

MSE

  1. How large can MSE get? How small? Does it measure the same thing that R-squared measures, or something different? Is it related to R-squared? How does it compare to the residual standard error? What units of measurement do both MSE and residual standard error have relative to the original data? In contrast, what units of measurement does R-squared have with respect to the original data? (For example, with the weather data, the units for high temperature are likely in degrees Fahrenheit. So are any of the MSE, residual standard error, or R-squared in the same units of degrees Fahrenheit?)

To close out our study, we have one last aspect of residuals. Remember way back when we got our SSE by adding up all the squares of the residuals? Well, it would be nice to know what their average is: are most of our predicted values close to the actual, or are half way too high and half way too low? To find that, we can use the Mean-Squared Error

\[MSE = \frac{SSE}{n-p}\] We dont quite divide the total by the number of residuals we have, instead we subtract from it the number of parameters we are using for our analysis. For this model, we have \(\beta_0\) and \(\beta_1\), so we divide by n-2. When we make predictions, this gives us a little more information about how likely our prediction is to be accurate. The result will be somewhere between our SSE and 0 (which would be a perfect regression)

The MSE is the square of our Residual Standard Error. That phrase, Residual Standard Error sounds like an estimate of the average error across the residuals, which is exactly what we were trying to get! Residual Standard Error, like residual, is measured like the y-axis, so for ours the RSE is in Degrees. Mean Squared Error is in \(Degrees^2\), which unfortunately doesnt make a lot of sense.
