I dont think this model is very accurate, because most of our observed low mileage values land above the expected value, and most of the high mileage cars were above it as well. Perhaps we can find a different equation that will better represent those edges.

boxCox(sub.lm)

With a center of .5, the BoxCox suggest using a square-root function on our analysis. That would look like this: \[\sqrt(Y'_i) = \beta_0 + \beta_1*x_i+\epsilon_i\] Ultimately though, we used the log() function because it worked just as well \[log(Y'_i)= \beta_0 + \beta_1*x_i+\epsilon_i,\text{ } or\text{ } Y'_i = e^{\beta_0 + \beta_1*x_i+\epsilon_i}\]

ggplot(subaru,aes(x=mileage,y=price)) +geom_point() + 
  stat_function(fun=function(x) (b.sqrt[1]+b.sqrt[2]*x)^2,aes(color='sqrt(Y)')) +
  stat_function(fun=function(x) exp(b.log[1]+b.log[2]*x),aes(color='log(Y)')) +
  labs(x='How Used is the Car? (Miles x 1000)',y='What\'s their Sale Price?',title='Close Call rn')

Now that we have our finalized decay equation

We have a few different options to maximizing the purchase of our care at its resale. We could have tried to flip the car right away, leave the mileage the same but make a profit. We could use the car until we reach the estimated resale price we got it for, then sell it for a net 0 cost. We can also drive it into the ground, or to a point where maintenance seems more costly than our use for it. \(~\)

Our formula for total estimate cost is: \[ \text{Purchase Cost per Mile} = \frac{\text{Mileage at Sell - Mileage at Purchase}}{\text{Price at Purchase - Price at Sell}}\] We already know the purchase price and mileage, and our regression lets us estimate the future sale price based on its mileage, so we can rewrite this equation as:

\[ \text{Purchase Cost per Mile} = \frac{\text{Mileage at Sell - 50K}}{\text{14.2K} - e^{\beta_0 + \beta_1*\text{Mileage at Sell}}}\] Lets graph it, and find the minimum!

curve((14.2-exp(b.log[1]+b.log[2]*x))/(x-50),xlim=c(50,150),ylab='Purchase Cost per Mile (in cents)',xlab='Car Miles when I Sell it (in 1000 miles)')
curve(0*x,add=TRUE,col='red')

Since we cant sell the car for less miles than we bought it for, our x axis starts at 50K miles. It seems that since the car was so cheap when we bought it, we dont start to lose money at the resale for another 83K miles after we bought it. That number never seems to bottom out, but Id guess that around 150K total (50K to start + 100K of driving) is when the maintenance becomes more than its worth, so well end there. At that point, the average cost per mile is

On the top line we see our default, linear regression. The biggest problem is with the fitted values, because the early and late observed values are consistently higher than expected,while the middle is lower. We can also see that a little bit in the qqPlot. In our new logarithmic regression, that fitted values problem disappears. However, by removing that, weve noticed that there is not as constant of a variance: low-mileage vehicles vary a lot more than the high-mileage. It was also present in the original regression, but a little more difficult to recognize.
